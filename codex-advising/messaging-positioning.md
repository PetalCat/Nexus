# Messaging and Positioning Research

## Goal

Reduce unnecessary trademark/brand risk in public-facing copy while staying accurate about what Nexus is.

This note is about wording and positioning, not legal advice.

## External Sources

- USPTO, "Likelihood of confusion": https://www.uspto.gov/trademarks/search/likelihood-confusion
- USPTO, "About Trademark Infringement": https://www.uspto.gov/page/about-trademark-infringement
- USPTO, "What is a trademark?": https://www.uspto.gov/trademarks/basics/what-trademark

## Recommended Positioning

- Describe Nexus as an independent self-hosted media frontend/orchestration layer.
- Say users connect their own services.
- Avoid implying affiliation, endorsement, sponsorship, or official support from third-party brands.
- Keep third-party names mostly in compatibility/setup contexts instead of headline marketing copy.

## Why This Matters

The most important practical trademark/messaging risk is not simple mention by itself. USPTO guidance centers risk around confusion about source, affiliation, or related goods and services. For Nexus, the larger messaging risk is copy that could imply:

- official affiliation
- endorsement
- sponsorship
- source confusion
- platform ownership or hosted-service responsibility

For Nexus, the most important distinction is:

- Nexus is the software layer
- users provide the services, infrastructure, and accounts they connect

## Good Public Language

- "Independent self-hosted media platform"
- "Frontend for user-configured services"
- "Unified interface across connected services"
- "Compatibility with supported integrations"
- "Works with user-supplied services and adapters"
- "Independent project, not affiliated with third-party platforms"

## Language to Avoid in Marketing Copy

- "Official support for ..."
- "For YouTube/Netflix/Nintendo"
- "Alternative to [consumer brand]" as a headline
- Anything that implies Nexus provides media, accounts, catalogs, subscriptions, anonymity, or legal compliance

## Distinction Between Public Copy and Technical Docs

Public copy should:

- lead with product capabilities
- avoid consumer-brand-led headlines
- avoid platform-comparison slogans

Technical/setup docs may still:

- list supported integrations
- explain required credentials/config
- mention third-party services when necessary for setup

That means brand names are not banned everywhere. They should be concentrated in operational contexts.

## User-Clarity Statements Worth Adding

- Nexus does not provide media, accounts, subscriptions, or hosted services.
- Users are responsible for the services they connect and how they use them.
- Nexus is not affiliated with or endorsed by third-party platforms or rights holders.
- Nexus does not guarantee privacy, anonymity, or legal compliance.

## Best Places to Surface These Statements

- public website footer or short disclaimer area
- install/getting-started docs
- onboarding/setup docs
- compatibility page

Avoid turning the homepage hero into legal copy. The disclaimer should support the messaging, not dominate it.

## Practical Wording Pattern

Preferred pattern:

1. describe what Nexus does
2. describe that users connect their own services
3. clarify non-affiliation briefly

Example structure:

- "Nexus is an independent self-hosted media frontend."
- "It connects to user-configured services and presents them in one interface."
- "Nexus is not affiliated with third-party platforms or content providers."

## Research Basis

- USPTO says trademark infringement turns on use likely to cause confusion, deception, or mistake about source.
- USPTO also makes clear that rights attach to how a mark is used with specific goods or services, not to a word in every context.
- For Nexus, that supports a practical policy of reducing brand-led marketing and avoiding implied affiliation rather than trying to purge every technical mention.
